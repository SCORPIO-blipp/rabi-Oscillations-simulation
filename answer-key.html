Rabi Oscillations Simulation

Answer Key
PART II Before You Start
1.In your own words, what is resonance? Give one example from everyday life that is not the swing.
Resonance is when a periodic driving force matches the natural frequency of a system, allowing energy to build up efficiently. Examples: a singer shattering a glass; a radio tuned to a station; a bridge oscillating in wind; pushing someone on a swing at the right moment.
2.A classical system driven at resonance grows in amplitude over time. Predict what will happen to a qubit driven at its resonant frequency. Will it keep gaining energy forever? Why or why not?
No. Unlike a swing, the qubit oscillates back and forth between |0⟩ and |1⟩ — it cannot go "above" |1⟩ since those are the only two states. The probability cycles between 0% and 100%; it never accumulates unboundedly.
3.What does it mean for a qubit to be in a 50/50 superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩?
The qubit is genuinely in both states simultaneously with equal probability. If measured, it will randomly collapse to |0⟩ or |1⟩ with 50% probability each. It is not secretly one or the other before measurement.

4.What pattern do you notice? How does changing Ω affect how fast the qubit oscillates?
A larger Ω produces a faster oscillation. The time to full inversion is π/Ω — doubling Ω halves the inversion time. At resonance (Δ = 0), the qubit always reaches 100% regardless of Ω.
5.At Δ = 0, does P(|1⟩) ever go above 100%? Why not?
No. Probability is bounded between 0 and 1 by definition. A qubit has only two states; 100% means complete inversion to |1⟩, which is the maximum physically possible excitation.

6.As you increase Δ, what happens to how high P(|1⟩) gets? Why can't the qubit fully flip when the frequency is off?
Max P(|1⟩) decreases as Δ increases. The driving field is no longer perfectly in sync with the qubit's natural frequency, so energy transfer is incomplete — some energy is wasted on the phase mismatch, and the qubit can't be fully inverted. Max excitation = (Ω/ΩR)².
7.Does a bigger Δ speed up or slow down the oscillation? Does that match what the ΩR formula predicts?
A bigger Δ speeds up the oscillation. Yes — the formula ΩR = √(Ω² + Δ²) shows that adding Δ always increases ΩR, which is the frequency of oscillation. A higher frequency means a shorter cycle time.

8.After a π/2 pulse with the laser off, you measure the qubit many times. What result do you expect on average? Why?
On average, |0⟩ half the time and |1⟩ half the time. The qubit is frozen in a 50/50 superposition; each measurement independently collapses it with equal probability to either state.
9.Apply two π pulses in a row starting from |0⟩. Predict the result first, then verify in the simulation.
a) Prediction:
The qubit should return to |0⟩ — two full inversions cancel out.
b) What the simulation showed:
P(|0⟩) = 100%, P(|1⟩) = 0%. The state returns to |0⟩, confirming that two π pulses are equivalent to the identity operation.
PART IV Analysis and Synthesis
10.How are Rabi oscillations similar to a swing? What is one way they are completely different?
a) Similarity:
Both involve a periodic driving force matched to a natural frequency causing maximum energy transfer.
b) Fundamental difference:
A swing's amplitude grows indefinitely with sustained driving; a qubit oscillates between exactly two states and can never exceed 100% excitation. The qubit also exists in superposition — it genuinely occupies both states simultaneously, which has no classical analogue.
11.If you were using this to flip a qubit in a real quantum computer, what two things could cause it to go wrong?
a) Factor 1:
Detuning (Δ ≠ 0) — if the drive frequency doesn't exactly match the qubit's transition frequency, full inversion is impossible and the gate will be imperfect.
b) Factor 2:
Mistimed pulse duration — stopping the drive at the wrong moment leaves the qubit at an intermediate state rather than fully in |1⟩. The pulse must last exactly π/ΩR.
12.The second is defined by 9,192,631,770 oscillations of a cesium-133 atom (a Rabi-type transition). Why would scientists define time using an atomic oscillation instead of a mechanical clock?
Every cesium-133 atom in the universe oscillates at exactly the same frequency — it is a physical constant, not subject to wear, temperature change, gravity, or manufacturing variation. Mechanical clocks degrade and drift; atomic transitions are identical and stable to one part in 1014, making them far more precise and reproducible as a time standard.
