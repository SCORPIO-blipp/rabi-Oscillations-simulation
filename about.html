What are Rabi
Oscillations?

A guided tour from playground swings to quantum computing, explaining one of the most elegant phenomena in modern physics. No prior quantum knowledge required.

01 — The big idea

Resonance: a force that works with nature

Almost everything in the universe vibrates at a natural frequency. A wine glass hums at a particular pitch. A bridge sways at a particular rhythm. A quantum particle transitions between energy levels at a particular rate. When an external force matches that natural frequency exactly, something remarkable happens: the system absorbs energy with extraordinary efficiency. This is resonance, and Rabi oscillations are resonance operating at the quantum scale.

"Rabi oscillations describe how a two-level quantum system, like a qubit, smoothly cycles between its two possible states when driven by an oscillating field tuned to its resonant frequency."

The phenomenon is named after Isidor Isaac Rabi, who discovered it in atomic beams in 1937 and won the Nobel Prize in Physics for it in 1944. Today it sits at the heart of NMR machines, atomic clocks, and every qubit in quantum computers.

02 — Classical interpretation

The playground swing: resonance you can feel

Imagine pushing a child on a swing. If you push randomly, most of your effort is wasted. But if you push at exactly the right moment, every time the swing reaches the back of its arc, the amplitude grows and grows. You are driving the system at its resonant frequency.

Analogy: the swing

Each push adds a little energy to the swing. Because the timing matches the swing's natural rhythm, energy accumulates constructively. Get the timing wrong, and the pushes cancel each other out, adding nothing. This is resonance in classical physics.

Other examples are everywhere. An opera singer shattering a glass by hitting its resonant frequency. A radio antenna tuned to pick up exactly one station's wavelength. The Tacoma Narrows Bridge, which collapsed in 1940 because wind drove it at its own resonant frequency.

In all these cases the core idea is the same: a periodic driving force matched to a system's natural frequency produces a large, growing response.

03 — Quantum interpretation

The qubit: oscillating between two realities

Now shrink the swing down to the size of a single atom or a superconducting circuit. At this scale, the rules of quantum mechanics take over, and something stranger happens.

A qubit, the basic unit of quantum information, can only exist in two states: |0⟩ (ground state, energy low) and |1⟩ (excited state, energy high). When a laser or microwave pulse is tuned to exactly match the energy gap between those two states, the qubit does not just gain amplitude like a swing. It cycles its identity back and forth between |0⟩ and |1⟩.

Quantum twist

Instead of getting "higher and higher" like a swing, the qubit smoothly rotates from fully |0⟩ to fully |1⟩ and back again. If you stop the drive halfway through the cycle, the qubit is in a perfect 50/50 superposition, simultaneously both states at once, until it is measured.

The rate at which this cycling happens is called the Rabi frequency (Ω), and it depends on how strong the driving field is. A more powerful laser means a faster oscillation. What makes this quantum rather than classical is superposition: between |0⟩ and |1⟩ the qubit is not secretly one or the other. It genuinely occupies both states with calculable probabilities that ripple back and forth sinusoidally. That is exactly what the simulator shows you.

04 — Off-resonance: detuning

What happens when the frequency is wrong?

In the simulator you can change the detuning (Δ), which is how far the driving field's frequency sits from the qubit's natural transition frequency. At zero detuning you have perfect resonance and the qubit fully inverts from |0⟩ to |1⟩ on every half-cycle.

Introduce detuning and two things happen. First, the oscillation speeds up because the effective driving frequency becomes the generalized Rabi frequency, combining both Ω and Δ. Second, the qubit never reaches a full |1⟩ state. The maximum excitation probability caps below 100%, like a swing being pushed slightly off-beat where some energy is wasted each time.

Generalized Rabi frequency
Omega_R = sqrt( Omega^2 + Delta^2 )

When Δ = 0, Ω_R collapses to Ω and you get full oscillation. As Δ grows, Ω_R increases but the amplitude of excitation shrinks. This is why frequency precision matters so much in quantum hardware. Even a small mismatch degrades the gate operation.

05 — Pulses and quantum gates

π pulses and superposition: the building blocks of quantum computing

In practice, physicists do not let the Rabi oscillation run freely. They apply precise timed bursts called pulses. The duration of the pulse determines exactly where in the oscillation cycle it stops, and therefore what state the qubit ends up in.

π/2 pulse

Creates superposition

  • Drive the qubit for exactly one quarter of a full Rabi cycle
  • Qubit lands at a 50/50 mix of |0⟩ and |1⟩
  • This is the quantum equivalent of a coin in mid-flip
  • Used to begin almost every quantum computation
π pulse

Inverts the state

  • Drive the qubit for exactly one half of a full Rabi cycle
  • Qubit flips completely: |0⟩ becomes |1⟩ and vice versa
  • This is the quantum NOT gate
  • The most fundamental single-qubit operation

When you press the π/2 pulse button in the simulator and turn the laser off, the qubit freezes in superposition. It holds both possibilities simultaneously. This frozen superposition is the raw resource that gives quantum computers their power: a qubit in superposition can explore multiple computational paths at once, which is the foundation of quantum speedup.

06 — Why it matters

From physics lab to quantum chip

Rabi oscillations are not just a theoretical curiosity. They are the physical mechanism behind some of the most important technologies built in the last century.

Atomic clocks, the most accurate timekeepers ever built, use Rabi transitions in cesium atoms to define the second. One second is defined as exactly 9,192,631,770 oscillations of a cesium-133 atom. Your GPS works because of this precision.

MRI machines use nuclear magnetic resonance, a direct application of Rabi oscillations in hydrogen nuclei, to image soft tissue in the human body without any ionizing radiation.

Quantum computers built by IBM, Google, and others implement every qubit gate by firing precisely timed microwave pulses that drive controlled Rabi oscillations. When you read about quantum computing milestones, Rabi oscillations are the mechanism running underneath every single operation.

07 — Glossary

Key terms, plainly defined

Qubit A quantum bit. Unlike a classical bit (always 0 or 1), a qubit can exist in a superposition of both states until measured.
|0⟩ and |1⟩ The two basis states of a qubit. The bracket notation is called Dirac notation, the standard shorthand in quantum mechanics.
Superposition A quantum state that is a combination of |0⟩ and |1⟩ simultaneously. Measurement forces it to resolve to one, with probabilities set by the superposition coefficients.
Rabi frequency (Ω) How fast the qubit oscillates between |0⟩ and |1⟩. Proportional to the amplitude of the driving field.
Detuning (Δ) The mismatch between the driving field's frequency and the qubit's natural transition frequency. Zero detuning means perfect resonance.
π pulse A pulse lasting exactly half a Rabi cycle. Flips the qubit from |0⟩ to |1⟩ or vice versa. The quantum NOT gate.
π/2 pulse A pulse lasting a quarter of a Rabi cycle. Puts the qubit into a 50/50 superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩.
Resonance When a driving force matches a system's natural frequency, producing maximum energy transfer. The same principle behind a playground swing, an MRI, and a qubit gate.
